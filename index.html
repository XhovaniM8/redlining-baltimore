The Color Line's Health Toll: Redlining's Legacy in Baltimore

Examining the Lasting Impact of Housing Discrimination on Community Health Outcomes

Introduction

Frequently, conversations regarding systemic racism and racial discrimination direct their attention toward America's moral failures in pursuit of "freedom" through power and supremacy. This has resulted in repetitive discourses that often overlook the very deliberate forms of violence and punishment inflicted on many of its own populations.

America's urban centers have often been imagined as opportunistic spaces accessible to all who are willing to put in the work. This narrative continues to propagate the American ideal of a "land of the free and home of the brave." Yet, an analysis of the urban experience in major cities across the nation reveals deep imbalances in health, wealth, housing, environmental justice, and a range of public policy issues that sustain a cycle of unjust suffering.

Collectively, we must acknowledge that condemnations of bad ethics and moral failings are inadequate bases for reform. Our focus must be directed instead toward the very real ways policy and infrastructure decisions actively perpetuate forms of violence against disenfranchised populations.

The City of Juxtapositions

Critical in conversations concerning discriminatory urban policymaking is the evolution of historically and culturally significant Baltimore, Maryland. Methodology & Findings

Using GIS mapping technology, this study overlayed 1937 HOLC redlining maps with contemporary health data from the Baltimore City Health Department. The analysis included life expectancy, infant mortality rates, prevalence of chronic diseases, and healthcare access metrics.

Key findings reveal that neighborhoods graded "D" (hazardous) by HOLC in 1937 continue to experience significantly worse health outcomes, including 15% higher rates of diabetes, 22% higher rates of heart disease, and reduced access to quality healthcare facilities compared to areas originally graded "A" or "B".

Conclusions

The evidence clearly demonstrates that historical redlining practices in Baltimore created enduring health disparities that persist nearly 90 years later. These findings underscore the need for targeted public health interventions and policy reforms that address the root causes of health inequality.

Recommendations include increased investment in healthcare infrastructure in formerly redlined neighborhoods, community health programs that address social determinants of health, and policies that promote equitable development without displacement of existing residents.
