Examining the lasting impact of housing discrimination on community health outcomes

ANTH 3135 Final

The Color Line's Health Toll: Redlining's Legacy in Baltimore

Introduction

Frequently, conversations regarding systemic racism and racial discrimination direct their attention toward America’s moral failures in pursuit of “freedom” through power and supremacy, resulting in repetitive discourses that overlook deliberate forms of violence and punishment inflicted on many of its own populations. America’s urban centers have often been imagined as opportunistic spaces accessible to all who are willing to put in the work, continuing to propagate the national opus of a “land of the free and home of the brave.” Yet analyses of the urban experience across major cities unveil imbalances in health, wealth, housing, environmental justice, and a host of public policy issues that sustain a cycle of unjust suffering.

Condemnations of bad ethics and moral failings are inadequate bases for reform. Our attention must be directed toward the concrete ways policy and infrastructure decisions actively channel violence onto disenfranchised populations.

Baltimore: A City of Contradictions

Central to conversations about discriminatory urban policymaking is the evolution of historically and culturally significant Baltimore, Maryland. Birthplace of both “The Star-Spangled Banner” and the Peabody Award–winning TV series The Wire, Baltimore exists in disquieting juxtapositions evident in its socioeconomic reality as much as its cultural contributions.

Originating as a key seaport city due to access to the Chesapeake Bay, Baltimore’s roles in trade, commerce, and industrialization have shaped this “city of firsts.” My focus, however, is on its being the first U.S. city to pass a residential segregation ordinance as part of a redlining experiment—creating a legacy of inequality and violence and setting a precedent for racial containment and segregationist urban policy nationwide.

The Origins of Systematic Segregation

The 1910 ordinance was enacted as retaliatory policy in response to the ineffectiveness of racial violence at preventing the encroachment of Black residents into white neighborhoods. When the ordinance was eventually ruled unconstitutional, zoning and other legal mechanisms replaced it to sustain racial containment. Redlining—through disinvestment and active disenfranchisement of majority-Black neighborhoods—exacerbated social and economic barriers to the point that these factors are observable in modern health outcomes.

Historic Grades, Present Outcomes

Through institutionalized segregation, the 13% of Baltimore graded “hazardous” by the HOLC in the 1930s overlaps with much of the city’s Black population—one of the largest urban African American communities in the U.S. In a study by Shuo Jim Huang and Neil Jay Sehgal, historical HOLC grades were related to present-day community statistical areas (CSAs) and health indicators such as life expectancy, child health, and age-differentiated mortality. Using map transformations, overlays, and statistical analysis, they established a clear correlation between HOLC “security” maps and contemporary health disparities across communities.

Huang notes: “Even after controlling for racial composition and median household income, CSAs categorized as red or yellow had a 5-year shorter life expectancy than CSAs categorized as green or blue,” adding that residents in redlined neighborhoods have reduced access to green spaces and physical activity—factors closely tied to chronic disease prevention and management. Lifelong exposure to racism and systemic hostility further contributes to adverse health outcomes. Although this project’s scope has limits, the correlation between HOLC grades and life expectancy warrants deeper evaluation of how security maps shape policy and infrastructure affecting Baltimorean health.

Quantifying the Impact

According to the Baltimore City Health Disparities Report Card (2013), among Baltimoreans earning under $75,000 annually, Black men and women suffered higher cardiovascular disease mortality than white residents. Black Baltimoreans also reported 1.82% higher burden of poor mental health and 1.62% more unmet healthcare needs compared to white Baltimoreans. One third of households earn under $25,000—a constraint that limits access to care, housing, and healthy food options, compounding risk even before accounting for substance use or violent victimization.

The report’s map of Avertable Premature Deaths estimates the deaths before age 75 that could have been avoided if every neighborhood enjoyed equal health opportunity comparable to the CSAs with the highest median incomes. In 26 of 49 CSAs (53%) with median incomes under $75,000, at least half of premature deaths were potentially avertable.

The Pandemic's Disproportionate Toll

Though the report card predates COVID-19, the pandemic laid bare how inequitable access to health opportunities devastates majority-Black communities. Pre-existing disparities intensified: acute and chronic stress, depression, and anxiety rose alongside barriers in access to resources, information, and unbiased care—contributing to elevated mortality.

A 2021 study on Black populations during the pandemic reported depressive symptoms including anger, chronic stress, anxiety, and fear for personal and familial health. Heightened stress activates adrenergic pathways and systemic inflammation, exacerbating underlying cardiovascular disease and increasing the risk of adverse events (Sims et al., 2020; Yancy, 2020). Coupled with Baltimore’s documented disparities, this nexus underscores the need to interrogate structural—not moral—failures that impede care.

Methodology & Synthesis

While this project’s constraints limit exhaustive analysis, the converging evidence—historical HOLC grades, present-day CSA health metrics, and socio-environmental indicators—supports a robust correlation between redlining and poor health. These patterns justify deeper evaluation of how past mapping practices continue to steer contemporary policymaking and infrastructure in ways that harm health.

In short: neighborhoods graded “D” (hazardous) by HOLC continue to experience markedly worse outcomes, including higher burdens of chronic disease and reduced access to health-promoting resources, compared with areas historically graded “A” or “B.”

Conclusions

Historical redlining in Baltimore created enduring health disparities that persist nearly a century later. Addressing these inequities demands targeted public health interventions and structural policy reforms that engage root causes.

Recommended actions include: investing in healthcare infrastructure in formerly redlined neighborhoods; funding community health programs that address social determinants (housing, food access, environmental quality); expanding high-quality green space and safe physical-activity corridors; and advancing equitable development policies that avoid displacing existing residents.
